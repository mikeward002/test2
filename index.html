What To Do After Someone Dies

A gentle step-by-step guide through the first 72 hours, when everything feels overwhelming and you need a steady hand to guide you forward.

You're Not Alone in This Moment

If you're reading this, someone important to you has died, and you're likely feeling overwhelmed, confused, or numb. This is completely normal. There's no "right" way to grieve, and there's no rush to do everything perfectly.

This guide will walk you through the practical steps that need attention in the first few days, while honoring the fact that your heart is processing something profound. Take what helps, leave what doesn't, and remember: you're doing better than you think.

If you're reading this late at night, unable to sleep, know that grief keeps its own schedule. These steps will still be here tomorrow.

First 24 Hours: The Essential Steps

Speaking with Funeral Homes: Scripts and Guidance

Funeral directors are experienced with grieving families and will be patient with you. Here are some scripts to help you feel more prepared:

Initial Call Script

"Hi, I need to arrange services for my [relationship]. [Name] passed away [when] at [location]. We don't have pre-arrangements. Could someone help guide me through the process? I'm feeling quite overwhelmed."

Questions to Ask

  • What are the total costs for different service options?
  • When do we need to make final decisions?
  • What can we decide later versus what needs to be decided now?
  • Do you handle obituary placement in newspapers?
  • What paperwork will you help us with?

Remember: You Can Take Time

It's perfectly acceptable to say: "This is a lot to process. Can I call you back in a few hours?" or "Can you email me these options so I can discuss them with my family?"

Days 2-3: Building on the Foundation

Writing an Obituary: A Gentle Guide

An obituary is both a public notice and a celebration of life. It doesn't need to be perfectâ€”it needs to be true to the person you're honoring.

Essential Information to Include:

  • Full name and age
  • Date and place of death
  • Immediate family members
  • Career highlights or life's work
  • Special interests or achievements
  • Service details
  • Memorial donation preferences

Writing Tips:

  • Start with basic facts, then add personality
  • Include something that made them unique
  • Ask family members for favorite memories
  • Aim for 200-500 words
  • Have someone proofread before submitting

Simple Template to Get Started:

[Full Name], age [age], of [city], passed away peacefully on [date] [location/circumstances if appropriate].

[He/She] was born in [location] to [parents' names] and [brief early life/education].

[Name] worked as [career] and was known for [key personality traits or achievements]. [He/She] enjoyed [hobbies/interests] and was passionate about [causes/activities].

[He/She] is survived by [family members]. [He/She] was preceded in death by [if applicable].

A [service type] will be held [date/time] at [location]. In lieu of flowers, the family suggests donations to [charity/cause].

Looking Ahead: Tasks for the Coming Weeks

These tasks don't need immediate attention, but having them organized can provide peace of mind as you move through the coming weeks.

Financial and Legal

  • Contact banks and financial institutions
  • Meet with estate attorney if needed
  • File life insurance claims
  • Contact Social Security Administration
  • Review and update your own will/beneficiaries

Personal and Household

  • Cancel subscriptions and memberships
  • Handle vehicle title and registration
  • Organize personal belongings (when ready)
  • Send thank-you notes for support received
  • Consider grief counseling or support groups

Emergency Self-Care Reminders

Physical Needs

  • Try to eat something, even if small
  • Stay hydratedâ€”keep water nearby
  • Rest when you can, even if sleep is difficult
  • Gentle movement can help (short walks, stretching)

Emotional Support

  • Accept help from othersâ€”let them bring meals
  • Talk to someone when you feel ready

You Are Doing Enough

Grief is not a problem to be solved or a task to be completed. It's love with nowhere to go, and it takes time to find new places for that love to live.
